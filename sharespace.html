Overview

Sharespace: Designing a portfolio platform that supports project-based work.

We were challenged by the educational non-profit Collective Shift to re-imagine ways that portfolios could be used for learners in and outside of the traditional school setting. As a solution, my team and I designed Sharespace - an educational platform that connects students with experts to receive feedback on in-progress and completed work. We focused on applying learning science principles and rich user insights to refine the scope of this challenge and to produce a well informed solution.

My Role: User Research, Document Design

Team Members: Nouf Aljowaysir, Yiran Buckley, Ian Go

Context: Learning Media Design, Fall 2015

Deliverables: Research Report, Design Case

Problem Space

"Digital portfolios are becoming an increasingly important educational approach in 21st Century Learning initiatives. Digital portfolio creation is both a learning process and product that documents student interests, skills, and achievements and enables youth to curate creative outputs and personal accomplishments over time and across learning settings. Digital portfolios can also be used by teachers to monitor and assess progress, and by academic institutions and potential employers in the application review process."

Collective Shift

We worked with Collective Shift to design an educational experience that utilized a portfolio system that was portable, modular, and could be shared. While our project was guided by the stakeholder goal of supporting portfolio practices, we proceeded to conduct user research to identify a specific problem that such a system would address.

Secondary Research

Literature Review

We conducted a literature review on emerging trends in education. Based on this, we identified Maker and project based classes as an ideal space to launch a portfolio system. Furthermore, we recognized reflection and critique as learning processes that would be necessary to support.

Competitive Analysis

Following the literature review, we conducted a competitive analysis on three systems: CritViz, Scratch, and Build-in-Progress. Research Insights

Based on the user research, we learned that students not only lacked feedback, but also lacked feedback from the sources that they valued the most: experts. Based on this we decided to pursue a social networking system. After speaking with pre-professionals, we realized that documentation and curation are distinct processes involved in the portfolio creation process. Because of this, we learned that different types of features would be necessary to both of these processes in our platform. Students need a space where they can document in-progress work for feedback and a space to curate their work for specific audiences.

Refining the Problem Space

Problem Statement

In an attempt to develop 21st century skills and competencies, a growing number of schools and out- of-school programs are implementing project-based or maker-based learning. While the new nature of this learning stands to help students pursue special skills and interests, students lack the resources to document this work for future use and the individualized support to progress successfully through projects.

Vision

We believe that building a system for high school students doing project-based work in school that would allow them to interact with experts of their fields of interest will lead learners to:

  • adopt documentation techniques
  • deepen engagement or discover interest in a subject
  • find resources to support the pursuit of such subjects
  • showcase these skills through documented artifacts

Synthesis

Personas

We further synthesized our research to create four different personas representing target learners. We organized these personas around levels of engagement and access to resources. We identified two user groups that we specifically wanted to impact: low resources and low engagement. In doing so, we could move forward to structure the system with these users in mind.

Scenarios

Using the personas, we developed a series of storyboards and identified specific activities for portfolio system to support. These included logistical processes such as on-boarding and portfolio specific activities such as documenting work and receiving feedback. We decomposed these storyboards into swimlanes, attributing each scene from the storyboard with different facets of the experience. Based on this, we identified concrete learning processes and system requirements that support the experience.

Prototyping

Iterations

We went through three iterations until developing a final deliverable prototype. We started off by constructing wireframes, creating a pen & paper prototype, and then constructing two versions of an InVision prototype.

Testing

After each iteration, conducted task analysis exercises with CMU undergraduates to test the efficacy of our design. Based on these results, we realized that more specific language was necessary and a thorough onboarding would be crucial.


Lo-Fi

Mid-Fi

Hi-Fi


In retrospect, we should have spent more time prototyping. While our team went through a really rich and deep design research process, this reduced the amount of time we had to actually create our system. As such, we rushed through the process of low fidelity prototying. While we rigorously tested our designs, we failed to spend enough time generating alternative designs. Looking back, it would have been valuable to consider alternative means of designing the system.
