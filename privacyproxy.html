Privacy Proxy

Empowering and educationg people to protect personal information on smartphone applications.

Overview

During the Fall of 2016, I worked as a UX Designer for Privacy Proxy - an android application that uses crowdsourced data to detect and notify users of personally identifiable information (PII) leaks. I conducted heuristic analysis to propose small scale re-designs and planned remote user testing sessions. I also had the opportunity to dabble in logo design and Android UI development.

This was my first time acting as the only UX Designer within a team. Because of this, I learned a lot about negotiating roles and taking sole responsibility over design choices. Furthermore, this way my first experience designing and developing for an Android application. Furthermore this was the first time I worked on a product that was at such a high fidelity. It was interesting to see how quickly changes could be implemented to the UI, but with obvious tradeoffs in regards to how complex designs coul be.

Heuristic Evaluation

By the time I joined Privacy Proxy, a pre-alpha version of the UI had already been developed. Because of this circumstance, I was unable to propose massive UI overhauls. However, I conducted a heuristic evaluation that resulted in small design changes concerning data visualization, micro UI interactions, and labelling.

Research

To test the efficacy of this product and the theory behind it, we conducted an IRB approved user research study. To prepare for this, I helped design a survey and task analysis. We launched the Privacy Proxy on Google Appstore and found participants to download the application. Users had Privacy Proxy installed on their phones for about a week. They were then asked to complete a post-test survey. Results are still being processed.
