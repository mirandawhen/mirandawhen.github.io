Overview

Peer Learning: Examining ways that peer interactions and technology drive learning.

During the Fall of 2016, I conducted a series of research projects that focused on the application and asessment of educational systems. I examined ways that peer interactions drive learning and identified design patterns that support to learning. Drawing upon theory from psychology, HCI, and learning science literature, I designed and evaluated different learning designs.

My Role: UX Researcher

Team Members: Anuraag Jain

Context: Designing Large-Scale Peer Learning Systems, Fall 2016

Study 1

Research Questions

How do groups brainstorm effectively? The primary goal of this study was to identify features for successful group brainstorming and to explain these features with principles from psychology and learning science.

Methods and Design

Three groups of students from creative disciplines were observed. Between these groups, factors such as experience, familiarity between members, and project type varied. Each observation session was followed by a contextual interview with one group member.

Findings

The observations reveal that students need support to better prepare themselves to conduct collaborative, creative work such as brainstorming. Students need the necessary resources, expertise, and intra-group dynamics to conduct successful collaborative work. In identifying different features of group brainstorming, more preparation is necessary to warrant the wide use of brainstorming.

Study 2

Research Question

How can peers help each other produce better notes? To address the cognitive burderns associated with realtime note-taking, we designed a new task flow that incorporated collaborative note-taking and peer feedback.

Methods and Design

To test the new task flow, we piloted an experiment using Mechanical Turk. We analyzed data by coding results by quality of notes, quality of feedback, and assessing learning with a post-task test.

Findings

Due to sample size, were were unable to conduct a robust statistical analysis. However, qualitative interviews demonstrated that our design had a positive impact on learning, with multiple participants stating that they would like to try the technique in class. Further research is underway.
